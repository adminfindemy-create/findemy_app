# 4. Users & Personas

Findemy is two-sided: **learners** (demand) and **academies** (supply). Both must grow together in the launch city for the marketplace to work (see the cold-start note at the end).

The MVP is designed for **all learner segments, across all age groups** — not a single niche. Below are the primary personas that the product must serve well.

## 4.1 Student / learner personas

### A. The adult passion learner
- **Who:** Working professionals and college students learning for enjoyment and personal growth (guitar after work, weekend dance, pottery, yoga).
- **Goals:** Find something nearby that fits a busy schedule; try before committing; not overpay.
- **Pains:** No time to call around; unsure which place is good; worried about wasting money on the wrong class.
- **Success:** Booked a trial in minutes, liked it, enrolled — with confidence it was the right pick.

### B. The parent booking for a child
- **Who:** A parent searching for skill classes for their kid (the **decision-maker is the parent**, the learner is the child).
- **Goals:** A safe, reputable academy; convenient timings/location; clear fees; ideally a trial to see if the child enjoys it.
- **Pains:** Hard to judge quality and safety; juggling logistics; comparing kids' batches across academies.
- **Success:** Found a trusted academy, the child attended a trial and liked it, enrolled in a recurring batch.

### C. The serious / aspiring learner
- **Who:** Someone pursuing a skill with intent — exams, auditions, competitions, or a path toward performing.
- **Goals:** Quality instruction, the right level/batch, a mentor with real experience.
- **Pains:** Distinguishing serious academies from casual ones; finding the right level; verifying mentor credibility.
- **Success:** Matched with an academy and coach that fit their ambition and level.

### D. The self-driven teen / young adult
- **Who:** School/college-age learners choosing and pursuing a skill themselves.
- **Goals:** Discover what's available, low-friction sign-up, social/peer relevance.
- **Pains:** Limited budget; wants to browse and decide independently.
- **Success:** Found and tried something cool without a lot of hassle or cost.

> Across all four, the shared job-to-be-done is: **"Help me find a place to learn [skill] that I can trust, evaluate, and try with low risk — then make it easy to commit."**

## 4.2 Academy personas

### E. The independent guru / small studio
- **Who:** A solo teacher or small academy, excellent at teaching, minimal online presence.
- **Goals:** A steady stream of interested local learners; fill batches; look credible online without effort.
- **Pains:** No marketing skill or budget; loses inbound interest with no system to manage it.
- **Success:** A clean profile, trial requests coming in, batches filling, students enrolling.

### F. The established academy / institute
- **Who:** A larger academy with multiple coaches, batches, and locations.
- **Goals:** More reach, organised intake, and a way to stand out beyond their own channels.
- **Pains:** Managing volume of trials/enrolments; consistency across batches.
- **Success:** Higher-quality leads and a manageable pipeline via the academy app.

> The academy job-to-be-done: **"Help me get discovered by serious local learners and convert them into enrolled students — with as little overhead as possible."**

## 4.3 Two-sided dynamics (cold start)

A marketplace is only useful when both sides are present:
- Learners won't come without enough **academy supply** to choose from.
- Academies won't invest attention without **learner demand**.

This is the central reason for the **single-city launch (Delhi-NCR)**: concentrate effort to reach critical density of academies and learners in one region before spreading thin across cities. Seeding strong supply (a good set of academies with real batches, reviews, and trial availability) is a prerequisite for a good learner experience at launch.
